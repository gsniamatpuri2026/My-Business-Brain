# VOICE DNA PROFILE

### SECTION 1: CONVERSATIONAL MARKERS

**Primary fillers:**

"Because" is the dominant connector in every answer — Gurmel explains the reason behind almost every statement rather than letting it stand alone: "because my job finished," "because of Covid-19 everything was stopped," "because I had two daughters," "because of financial problems" (used repeatedly across Q1, Q3, Q8, Q9, Q12). This is a heavy, constant pattern — nearly every other sentence carries a "because" clause.

"So" is used as a conclusion-bridge: "so the situation was very tough," "so this was the situation when I thought about my work," "so money is very important," "so then I thought." Heavy user — appears in nearly every answer as the hinge between cause and consequence.

"Like" is used as an example-introducer rather than a true filler: "like seeking help from friends and relatives," "like when I bought my first property in 2017," "like the requirements and the process," "like in Portugal, EB5, Greece." Moderate, consistent use.

**Transition phrases:**
"That's why..." is his signature pivot to a conclusion or justification — it appears across multiple separate answers: "That's why, instead of taking the huge amount of money, we advised him" (Q3), "That's why I took this decision to do it" (Q7), "That's why whatever I do, people like it, so that's why people say what I do is good" (Q11).

"Finally..." marks the resolution point of a struggle: "finally we had communication with the developer," "Finally, however, the deal was successfully completed" (Q1), "but finally I lost a lot of money" (Q6).

**Sentence openers:**
Overwhelmingly "I" — direct, first-person declarative openers: "I was working," "I lost my job," "I think," "I want," "I took many risks," "I had a female client." Secondary opener is "This was..." used to anchor a moment in time: "This was 2019 when Covid-19 started," "This was also when I thought," "This was the most common mistake." A third recurring opener is "But" used to pivot against a difficulty: "But ultimately, even through the COVID-19, I was still able to pay the installments."

### SECTION 2: SENTENCE PATTERNS

**Average length:** Long — his sentences run 20–35 words on average, built from multiple clauses stacked together with conjunctions rather than broken into shorter statements.

**Preferred structure:**
- Longer flowing thoughts (20+ words) dominate: "It was very difficult to get the property deal done, but with the help of my trust in God, with the blessing of God, finally we had communication with the developer to give some exemption." (Q1)
- Medium explanatory sentences also appear regularly: "I want to be fair to everyone. I want to not do any injustice to anybody." (Q3)
- Short punchy declarations are rare but land hard when used, almost always to state a stark fact: "I lost my job. There was no work." (Q1) and "No problem" (Q8).
- Embedded lists inside a sentence: "you should have all aspects of your life fulfilled: financially, socially, family, family, religious" (Q12) — note the doubled word "family," a natural emphasis repetition rather than an error to smooth out.

**Sentence variety:** He does not naturally vary length for effect — long explanatory sentences are the default, occasionally broken by a short, blunt sentence that lands as emphasis precisely because it interrupts the longer rhythm.

**Opening patterns:** "I [verb]..." / "This was/is..." / "But..."

### SECTION 8: RED FLAGS — WHAT THEY'D NEVER SAY

**Language they avoid:**
- Corporate jargon: never uses words like "synergy," "leverage," "circle back," "low-hanging fruit," "best-in-class," or "game-changer" anywhere across 15 answers.
- Hype or exaggeration: never overstates an outcome with words like "incredible," "insane," "world-class" — outcomes are described plainly ("successfully completed," "successfully approved").
- Casual slang or jokes: tone stays earnest and sincere throughout, even in lighter moments ("No problem," Q8, is as casual as it gets).

**Specific violations (7):**

✗ NEVER write: "We're disrupting the immigration industry."
Why: He never frames his work as disruption or innovation for its own sake — credibility comes from doing things "properly" and "in the right way" (Q10, Q11), not from claiming novelty.

✗ NEVER write: "I'm a visionary leader in this space."
Why: He never makes a direct claim about himself; credibility is always built through a specific client story, never a self-description.

✗ NEVER write: "Here are 5 quick tips for your visa application."
Why: He never opens with a listicle or hook before context — every list (Q11, Q12) appears after the reasoning has already been laid out in prose.

✗ NEVER write: "Honestly, this is a no-brainer."
Why: "Honestly" for him is reserved for describing his actual conduct toward a client ("I honestly advised him," Q3), not as a casual conversational filler.

✗ NEVER write: "Let's circle back on this."
Why: No corporate-meeting language appears anywhere in his natural voice.

✗ NEVER write: "I crushed it" / "we smashed the target."
Why: Outcomes are always credited partly to effort, partly to circumstance or faith ("with the blessing of God," Q1) — never claimed as a solo triumph.

✗ NEVER write: A statistic or bold claim as an opening line.
Why: Every answer opens with situation and context first, never with a number or claim — even Q14, about industry trends, opens with "My industry is changing very fast" before any specifics.

**Structural violations:**
- Never opens with a statistic, hook, or claim before establishing the situation — context always comes first.
- Never uses a bullet list as the opening move of a thought — lists only appear after the reasoning has been explained in prose first (Q11, Q12).
- Never ends an answer on an open question or unresolved note — always closes with a stated conclusion or lesson.
