## SECTION 3: THE CORE PROBLEM THEY WANT SOLVED

**Problem 1:** They've spent twenty or thirty years building a life, and it all sits on a single foundation — one country, one passport, one set of rules they had no hand in writing and no power to change.

**Problem 2:** They've been burned before. Consultants who quoted low, promised fast, went quiet after the invoice cleared, or submitted a poorly prepared file that left a record and a mess to clean up.

**Problem 3:** They feel like the system was never built for people like them — that doors open differently depending on which passport you carry, and their children will inherit their limitations unless someone acts now.

**Why they haven't solved it yet:**
They've tried. Some went to large firms, got handed to a junior, and never felt anyone actually read their file. Others did it alone — smart people who researched thoroughly but discovered that immigration is a judgement problem, not a research problem. Knowing a programme exists is not the same as knowing whether it suits your profile, your source of funds, your family structure, your long-term plan. What the market has never given them is someone who looks at the full picture, tells the truth, and stays on the case from start to finish.

---

## SECTION 4: WHAT THEY WANT INSTEAD

**Tangible outcome:** Approved residency or citizenship status; a complete, documented file that belongs to them; full clarity on renewals, travel rights, and tax considerations.

**Emotional outcome:** To stop carrying the weight of not knowing. To feel settled — not reactive. To plan a decade ahead instead of waiting to see what next year brings.

**Identity outcome:** From exposed to prepared. From dependent on circumstances to someone who has options. Someone who thinks ahead, plans properly, and has the security to prove it.

## SECTION 8: CONTENT DIRECTION FROM ICP

- **What topics resonate:** The cost of waiting; how immigration decisions are actually read by case officers; the difference between knowing a programme and knowing if it's right; what "source of funds" documentation really requires; stories of families who moved from vulnerable to prepared.
- **What tone connects:** Direct. Substantive. No cheerleading. No buzzwords. The tone of someone who has seen this industry from the inside and is tired of watching people get hurt by it.
- **What proof they need:** A client story where things went wrong before and were put right properly. Evidence of working personally on cases — not delegating. Demonstrable knowledge of how case officers actually assess files.
- **What to avoid saying:** "Fast approvals", "guaranteed outcomes", "100% success rate", "hassle-free", anything that sounds like what the consultants they've already been burned by would say.
